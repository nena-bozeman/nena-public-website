## 2007 — The Neighborhoods Program

The City of Bozeman's [Neighborhoods Program](https://www.bozeman.net/departments/administration/communications-engagement/neighborhoods-program) was established in 2007 to enhance communication between residents and the City, foster partnership and consensus among diverse interests, offer opportunities for public participation in city processes, and recognize residents' stewardship of their neighborhoods.

The program rests on two foundations:

- **[City Charter Article 4.06 — Neighborhood Associations](https://library.municode.com/mt/bozeman/codes/code_of_ordinances/459978?nodeId=PTICH_ARTIVDEOFAG_S4.06NEAS)** establishes Neighborhood Associations (NAs) and the Inter-Neighborhood Council (INC) at the charter level — the constitutional layer of Bozeman's municipal government.
- **[Neighborhood Recognition Ordinance](https://library.municode.com/mt/bozeman/codes/code_of_ordinances?nodeId=PTIICOOR_CH2AD_ART5BOCO_DIV7NEAS)** (Chapter 2, Article 5, Division 7 of the municipal code) provides the operational mechanics — how groups of residents organize, propose boundaries, and gain official recognition as an NA.

### The Inter-Neighborhood Council (INC)

The [INC](https://www.bozeman.net/departments/administration/communications-engagement/neighborhoods-program/interneighborhood-council) is a citizen-led advisory board to the City Commission, bringing together delegates from each officially recognized neighborhood association. It acts as a collaborative forum where associations share information, amplify local concerns, and offer collective recommendations to the City Commission and city staff. The INC operates under its own bylaws (approved by ordinance), is supported by a designated city staff liaison, and generally meets the 2nd Thursday of each month from 4:30–6:00 p.m. at City Hall.

### NENA's place in the system

The [Northeast Neighborhood Association (NENA)](https://www.nenabozeman.org/about) is one of Bozeman's 13 active neighborhood associations. NENA's boundaries are North Broadway Street to the east, North Grand Avenue to the west, East Mendenhall Street to the south, and East Oak Street to the north. As a recognized NA, NENA seats a delegate on the INC and participates in the structured public-engagement framework the 2007 program created.

### Why the charter placement matters

The NAs and INC exist in *both* the City Charter and the municipal code. This dual placement is structurally significant: anything that lives only in ordinance can be repealed by a simple commission majority, while charter language requires going back to voters via a charter amendment. Article 4.06 is what gives the neighborhood program a protected status in Bozeman's governance — not just a city program operating at the pleasure of whichever commission is currently seated.
