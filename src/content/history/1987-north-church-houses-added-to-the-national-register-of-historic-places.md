**North Church Avenue historic houses**

Four landmark residences on North Church Avenue between Villard and Peach Streets document the northeast neighborhood's working-class roots and the Northern Pacific Railroad's influence on north-side Bozeman. Three were listed individually on October 23, 1987 as part of the [Bozeman Multiple Resource Area](https://npgallery.nps.gov/NRHP/GetAsset/NRHP/64000424_text); a fourth was documented in the same nomination inventory.

| Address | Name | Built | Style |
| --- | --- | --- | --- |
| 216 N. Church | [Newman House](https://npgallery.nps.gov/AssetDetail/NRIS/87001835) | c. 1890–91 | Queen Anne (tri-gable) |
| 224 N. Church | [Busch House](https://historicmt.org/items/show/503) | c. 1895 | Queen Anne |
| 223 N. Church | Vreeland House | 1888, 1900 | Queen Anne / Eclectic |
| 510 N. Church | [Bohart House](https://historicmt.org/items/show/579) | 1889 | Vernacular brick cottage |

The **Newman House** was designed by Thomas Chester and Stephen Newman. The **Busch House** was home to Apollo J. Busch, a Northern Pacific section foreman who later helped build the Gallatin Valley Electric Railway—the first electric line west of Chicago. The **Bohart House**, built near the northern edge of residential Bozeman in 1889, marked the end of the 1880s building boom triggered by the railroad's arrival.

North Church's extra-wide boulevard once carried electric trolley cars from the depot to destinations across town. Together, these houses illustrate how railroad workers and their families shaped the character of the north side—modest, varied, and closely tied to the city's early industry.
