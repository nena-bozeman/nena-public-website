In 1933, the **Missoula Mercantile Company** (MISCO) added a towering grain elevator to the Bozeman skyline at **700 North Wallace Avenue**, at the intersection of Wallace and East Cottonwood Street. Situated beside the Northern Pacific Railroad line and the Bon Ton Flour Mill, the building expressed the wholesaler's confidence in Montana agriculture even as the Great Depression gripped the region. According to the [1987 National Register nomination](https://npgallery.nps.gov/NRHP/GetAsset/89905238-3149-4052-a28d-bcb1c32521f2), it was one of very few grain elevators constructed between Minneapolis and Seattle during those years.

## How the elevator was built

Although concrete elevators had grown common after 1920, the MISCO elevator was built with the older **wooden crib technique**: carpenters stacked planks two inches tall and six inches wide, joining them with spikes and overlapping them at the corners to form walls strong enough to hold thousands of tons of grain. Narrower two-by-fours carried the crib walls up to the shoulder; a [warehouse portion was added a year or two after the elevator itself](https://npgallery.nps.gov/NRHP/GetAsset/89905238-3149-4052-a28d-bcb1c32521f2). The nomination describes it as the best remaining example of wooden cribbed grain elevator construction in Bozeman.

Farmers drove loaded trucks onto a scale above the "boot," where grain was dumped and lifted by a vertical belt-and-bucket conveyor to the cupola, then distributed to storage bins until elevator operators loaded it into railway freight cars for shipment out of state. [Historic Montana](https://historicmt.org/items/show/595) summarizes this workflow from the Montana National Register sign program.

## Grain trade on the northeast side

Gallatin Valley farms had long supplied local millers; surplus hard wheat did not ship to markets outside Montana until 1907. By 1912 Bozeman had a dozen elevators with a combined capacity of roughly 600,000 bushels—nearly half the county's grain cleaning, storing, and handling, as summarized in the [NRHP nomination](https://npgallery.nps.gov/NRHP/GetAsset/89905238-3149-4052-a28d-bcb1c32521f2) from early county surveys. Even after that boom, the MISCO elevator—built when Walter Teslow managed the Missoula Mercantile's eastern division—remained a physical link between outlying farms and the city's commerce.

## Later owners and uses

When Teslow left the company in 1946, he returned to the grain business on his own. In **1956**, after the Missoula Mercantile exited grain handling, Teslow bought the MISCO elevator—one of eighteen he operated across Montana and Idaho. **Peavey Company** acquired it in 1966; **Eric Anderson** purchased it in 1973 and used it to mix mineral feed supplements, with part of the warehouse leased as a recording studio. This ownership chain is documented in the [NRHP nomination](https://npgallery.nps.gov/NRHP/GetAsset/89905238-3149-4052-a28d-bcb1c32521f2) and on [Historic Montana](https://historicmt.org/items/show/595).

The **Thompson family** later bought the empty, deteriorating structure and painstakingly restored it, as described on [Misco Goods — The Mill](https://www.miscogoods.com/the-mill). Today the building houses [Misco Goods](https://www.miscogoods.com/), the [Misco Mill Gallery](https://www.miscomill.com/), and [2 Rivers Collection](https://www.2riverscollection.com/)—a neighborhood anchor that still speaks to Bozeman's industrial and agricultural roots.

## National Register listing

The MISCO Grain Elevator was listed on the **National Register of Historic Places** on [October 23, 1987](https://npgallery.nps.gov/AssetDetail/NRIS/87001831) (NRHP #87001831) for its significance in agriculture and architecture.
